  • Vedic Maths children calculate 3–5× faster mentally than peers using traditional methods.
  • That speed transfers into school maths, science, and competitive exams.
  • Coding teaches structured thinking — break a big problem into small, testable steps.

  • Parents love that we're not blindly copying the West — we're connecting our wisdom to modern tools.

f. Affordable, Inside School

  • No need to send the child anywhere extra — it happens inside their existing school day / Saturday.
  • No commute, no extra time pressure, no extra tuition fatigue.

4What the Student Walks Away With — Our 4 Levels

Use this when a parent asks: "But what will my child actually learn?"

T.H.Y.N.K is structured as a 4-level journey — one level per academic year, from Grade 4 to Grade 7. Each level has a name that the child carries proudly.

LevelNameTrackGrade

Level 1 — Young Explorer (Beginners Basic 1 · Grade 4)

  • First introduction to Scratch — drag-and-drop block coding.
  • Build their first 2 working games — Guess-My-Number & Times-Table Race.
  • Master sequencing, loops, basic variables — the building blocks of every programming language.
  • Vedic foundations: Ekadhikena Purvena (squares ending in 5), times-table shortcuts.
  • Outcome: the child no longer fears maths or a computer — they play with both.

Level 2 — Young Creator (Beginners Basic 2 · Grade 5)

  • Deepen Scratch — conditionals, operators, multiple sprites & costumes.
  • Build 3 more projects — Nikhilam Subtraction Wizard, Urdhva Multiplier, and a Final Project of their own design.
  • Vedic: Nikhilam (subtraction near a base), Urdhva-Tiryagbhyām (vertically & crosswise multiplication).
  • First guided AI conversations — using AI as a thinking partner, not an answer machine.
  • Outcome: the child can imagine a small game and build it on their own.

Level 3 — Young Innovator (Advanced 1 · Grade 6)

  • Jump from blocks to the real world — typing actual code.
  • Learn HTML & CSS — the two languages that shape every website on the internet.
  • Build their first multi-page personal website.
  • Right-click "Inspect" on any website and understand what they're seeing.
  • Vedic: revisit and master earlier sutras; introduce Paravartya Yojayet (division shortcut).
  • Outcome: the child stops being a consumer of the internet and becomes a builder of it.

Level 4 — Young Leader (Advanced 2 · Grade 7)

  • Add JavaScript — the same language Google, Instagram and YouTube run on.
  • Build a real, published website by Week 24 — interactive, with logic, that the parent can visit on their phone.
  • Use AI safely and effectively for code generation, debugging and explanation.
  • Vedic: integrate sutras into JavaScript-based calculators and quiz apps.
  • Present a 3-minute "Demo Day" to peers and parents — public speaking + product thinking.
  • Outcome: a real portfolio + the confidence to lead a tech project at school or beyond.

Across All 4 Levels — Common Threads

  • Learn to talk to AI properly — frame prompts, refine answers, treat AI as a thinking partner (we teach this safely; teacher guide has a full chapter on age-appropriate AI prompting).
  • Speak with confidence — every session ends with the child explaining their work to the class.
  • A certificate at each level — Young Explorer, Young Creator, Young Innovator, Young Leader — that the child earns and keeps.

7Anticipated Objections — How We Respond

Both of us must give the same answer. Memorise these.

We already do Vedic Maths in our school.
"Wonderful — that means your children are already half-way there. T.H.Y.N.K is not a Vedic Maths replacement; it's the coding application of what they already know. We sit on top of your existing programme and amplify it."
Our computer teacher doesn't know Vedic Maths.
"Perfectly fine. Our Teacher Guide assumes zero Vedic Maths background. The one-day training covers everything they need. The teacher learns alongside the students for the first 4 sessions — and then they're confident for life."
We already teach coding in school.
"Most schools teach Office, basic typing, or theory of computers. T.H.Y.N.K teaches building things — actual games, actual websites, actual AI use. There's no overlap; if anything, your existing IT class becomes the prep, and T.H.Y.N.K becomes the showcase."
Why only Scratch? Isn't that just drag-and-drop — not real programming?
"Fair question — and the answer surprises most parents. Scratch is used only in Grades 4 and 5, and it is chosen very deliberately. From Grade 6 the child moves to real typed code — HTML, CSS — and in Grade 7 to JavaScript, the same language Google, Instagram and YouTube run on. So by the end of the 4-year journey, the child is writing production-grade code by hand.

Why Scratch first? Scratch was built at MIT's Media Lab for exactly this age group. It is used in over 150 countries and in the CBSE, ICSE and IB coding curricula. It is not a toy — it is a serious teaching tool that removes the one thing that kills 90% of young coders: typing errors and syntax frustration.

A 9-year-old asked to type for(i=0;i<10;i++) will give up in a week. The same 9-year-old dragging a "repeat 10 times" block learns the exact same concept — loops — and builds a working game the same day. The thinking is identical; only the friction is removed.

Every core concept of programming — sequencing, loops, conditionals, variables, events, functions — is taught in Scratch. When the child then meets JavaScript in Grade 7, they are not learning programming for the first time; they are only learning a new way to write what they already know. That is why our Grade 7 children pick up JavaScript in weeks, not years.

Think of it like music: no one hands a 9-year-old a violin on day one. They start with a keyboard where the notes are already labelled. Two years later, the violin feels natural. Scratch is that labelled keyboard for coding."
Why not just use free YouTube tutorials?
"Because YouTube has no structure, no assessment, no teacher support, and no Vedic connection. A child clicking random videos learns scattered tricks; a child following T.H.Y.N.K builds a portfolio of 5 working projects in a year, with a teacher to debug with them."
What about screen time concerns?
"We agree completely — and that's exactly why we're here. The program is 35 minutes a week, supervised, building something meaningful. We're replacing 35 minutes of consumption with 35 minutes of creation."
₹3,600 per child — why this price?
"It's deliberately under ₹150 a session. Less than one private tuition class. We've kept it affordable so no parent has to choose between this and another activity. And 20% comes back to the school — so it's not a cost, it's a partnership."
What if the program flops mid-year?
"We have a midpoint review at Week 12 — you give us honest feedback, we adjust. We also offer a pilot cohort at no cost. If our promises don't show in 12 weeks, we walk away. We're confident enough to put that in writing."
My child doesn't like coding / My child says coding is boring.
"Completely fair — and almost always, the child is right about what they've seen so far. Most children who say this have only ever seen coding as a black screen with strange commands, or a textbook chapter on 'parts of a computer'. Neither of those is coding.

T.H.Y.N.K starts with the child dragging colourful blocks to make a cat dance, then move, then ask questions. Within the first 35 minutes the child has built something that responds to them on screen. We've yet to meet a child who didn't smile at that moment.

Think of cricket — no child says 'I hate cricket' before ever holding a bat. 'I don't like coding' usually means 'I've never been shown coding I can like'.

Also — T.H.Y.N.K is not pure coding. It is coding plus Vedic Maths plus game-making plus AI conversation. Even a child who later chooses not to pursue coding walks away with sharper mental maths, structured thinking and confidence with technology — skills they will use in any career.

Give us 4 sessions. If your child still says 'boring' after that, we will personally meet you and review what's not working. We've never needed that meeting yet."
My child is going to study Commerce / Arts — why this program?
"This is exactly the right question — and our honest answer often surprises parents.

By 2030, every Commerce field is digital: a CA uses AI for audit, a banker uses Python for analysis, an entrepreneur builds their own website on day one, a marketer reads dashboards every morning. Commerce without digital fluency in 2030 = Commerce without a calculator in 1990.

Arts is even more affected: filmmakers use AI for editing, designers use Figma + code together, writers publish on websites they build themselves, musicians produce on software. The world's top creators today are part artist, part technologist.

T.H.Y.N.K is not a 'become a programmer' program. It is a 'become digitally fluent and mentally sharp' program — equally useful for a future CA, a future fashion designer, a future psychologist, a future filmmaker.

And the Vedic Maths side directly serves Commerce — accounts, statistics, finance, mental arithmetic become effortless. Structured coding-style thinking directly serves Arts — planning essays, projects, presentations, storyboards becomes natural.

Finally — your child is 9 or 10 today. They genuinely do not yet know what they will choose at 16. The worst thing any of us can do is close doors based on a guess. T.H.Y.N.K keeps every door — Science, Commerce, Arts, Design, Entrepreneurship — wide open."

Pitching Discipline — Aradhana & Pranav

  1. Open with Plan A only. Pitch it as our standard offering. Do not pre-announce alternatives — that signals weakness.

10Words / Phrases — Use & Avoid

USE these — they land well

  • "Future-ready" / "Future-proof"
  • "From consumer to creator"
  • "Indian heritage, modern code"
  • "Plug-and-play for the school"
  • "Audit-ready, NEP-aligned"
  • "Partnership, not a vendor deal"
  • "Their first portfolio of real projects"
  • "Thinking partner, not answer machine" (about AI)
  • "By Week 24, the child will…"

AVOID these — they raise red flags

  • "Easy" / "Simple" — sounds shallow. Use "structured" or "step-by-step" instead.
  • "Everyone is doing this" — they're not, and the principal knows it.
  • "Coaching class" / "Tuition" — we're a program, not a tuition.
  • "Cheap" — we're affordable.
  • "Replace your teacher" — we empower the teacher.
  • Over-promising specific exam scores — promise skill, never rank.
